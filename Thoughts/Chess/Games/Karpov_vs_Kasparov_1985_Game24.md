# ♟️ Karpov vs. Kasparov (1985) - Game 24
## *The Game That Changed the World — QGD Under Maximum Pressure*

**Event:** World Championship 1985 (Game 24 — The Finale!)
**Location:** Moscow
**Date:** November 9, 1985

---

## 📜 Historical Context

This is arguably the most important game in chess history. Going into Game 24, the score was Karpov 13–11. Karpov needed just a draw to retain his title. Kasparov needed to win at all costs.

The pressure was unimaginable. The entire Soviet chess establishment was behind Karpov. Kasparov, at 22, was fighting against the system itself. He chose the **Queen's Gambit Declined — Tartakower Variation** as Black — the SAME variation you play!

What happened next was the greatest clutch performance in chess history. Kasparov won a game he absolutely had to win, with the whole world watching.

4. **The ...c5 Break:** Just like in the Caro-Kann, the ...c5 break is critical in the QGD. Kasparov times it perfectly.

## 📉 The Critical Moments

### Moment 1: The Opening Choice (Moves 1-7)

Kasparov chose the Tartakower because it's the most fighting variation of the QGD. The ...b6/Bb7 plan gives Black real chances for counterplay.

**Your Takeaway:** When you need to fight for a win as Black, the Tartakower is your weapon. Don't switch to something unfamiliar under pressure.

### Moment 2: The ...c5 Central Break (Move 15...c5!)

```
15...c5!
```

This is THE move. Just as in the Caro-Kann Advance (see [[Timman_vs_Karpov_1988]]), the ...c5 break liberates Black's position. Kasparov times it when his pieces are ready.

### Moment 3: The Exchange Sacrifice (Move 28...Rxc3!)

Kasparov sacrifices the Exchange (Rook for Bishop) to destroy Karpov's pawn structure AND gain a passed d-pawn. This is a *positional* Exchange sacrifice — identical to Fischer's Rxf6 in [[Fischer_vs_Spassky_1972]].

## 🎯 Strategic Themes

### 1. The Tartakower Setup In Action

This game is the most famous practical demonstration of your main opening:

```
1. d4 d5 2. c4 e6 3. Nc3 Nf6 4. Bg5 Be7 5. e3 h6 6. Bh4 b6
```

[♟️ View the Tartakower tabiya](https://lichess.org/editor/rnbq1rk1/p1ppbpp1/1p2pn1p/3p4/2PP3B/2N1P3/PP3PPP/R2QKBNR_w_KQ_-_0_7)

Key ideas Kasparov demonstrates:
- **...b6 + ...Bb7:** The light-squared bishop FINALLY becomes active
- **...h6 before ...b6:** Prevents Bg5xb8 (a common beginner mistake)
- **Nxd5 recapture with the Knight:** Keeps the position dynamic

**Your Checklist (from [[My Repertoire - QGD and Caro-Kann#📝 Tartakower Checklist]]):**
- [x] Play ...h6 BEFORE ...b6 ✓
- [x] Fianchetto the bishop to b7 ✓
- [x] Aim for ...c5 break ✓
- [x] Don't rush ...dxc4 ✓
- [x] If White plays cxd5, recapture with Knight (Nxd5!) ✓

### 2. Practical Decision-Making (Time Management)

In a must-win game, Kasparov didn't play the "best" move according to the computer — he played the most PRACTICAL move. There's a difference:

| Computer's "Best" Move | Practical Move |
|:-----------------------|:---------------|
| Maximizes evaluation | Maximizes confusion |
| Often drawish | Creates imbalances |
| Requires precise play from both sides | Makes opponent think hard |

**Your Time Management Lesson:**
- **Opening (moves 1-12):** Play quickly! You know these moves. Don't burn time thinking. Budget: **2 minutes max** in 10+0.
- **Middlegame critical decisions (moves 13-25):** This is where to invest time. Kasparov thought longest on ...c5! and the Exchange sacrifice. Budget: **5-6 minutes**.
- **Endgame/Technical Phase (moves 26+):** Play on instinct and principles. Don't calculate everything — evaluate. Budget: **2 minutes**.

```

┌──────────────┬──────────┐
│ Phase        │ Time     │
├──────────────┼──────────┤
│ Opening      │ 2 min    │
│ Middlegame   │ 6 min    │
│ Endgame      │ 2 min    │
└──────────────┴──────────┘
```

### 3. The Power of the Exchange Sacrifice

Kasparov plays 28...Rxc3! — giving up a Rook for a Bishop. This creates:
- A monster passed d-pawn
- Ruined White's pawn structure
- Active piece play

**This is the same theme as:**
- Fischer's Rxf6 in [[Fischer_vs_Spassky_1972]]
- Karpov's Rxe6 and Rxg6 in [[Karpov_vs_Topalov_1994]]

**Your Takeaway:** Exchange sacrifices (Rook for minor piece) are one of the most important strategic weapons at the intermediate level. When your opponent's pawn structure will be ruined, consider giving up the Exchange!

### 4. Playing for a Win, Not a Draw

Kasparov's approach teaches a critical mindset lesson:
- He didn't play the "safest" moves
- He deliberately chose positions where BOTH players could go wrong
- He trusted his tactical ability (just like you should trust your 1807 puzzle rating!)

**Your Takeaway:**
- Stop playing for draws with Black! The Tartakower gives real winning chances
- When you're better, play for the win — don't settle for a "safe" result
- Trust your tactical strength. Your puzzle rating is much higher than your game rating

## 🚦 Key Takeaways

1. **The Tartakower Works at Every Level.** From 1265 to 2800, this opening gives Black fighting chances. Trust your opening choice.

2. **...c5 is the Liberating Break.** In both the Caro-Kann and the QGD, ...c5 is the key move. Time it when your pieces are ready — usually by move 12-15.

3. **Exchange Sacrifices Win Games.** Rook for Bishop is often worth it when you get a passed pawn and active pieces. Don't be afraid of "losing material."

4. **Manage Your Time in Three Phases.** Opening = 2 min, Middlegame = 6 min, Endgame = 2 min. Stick to this budget religiously.

5. **Play Practically, Not Perfectly.** Don't look for the computer's best move. Look for the move that gives your opponent the hardest time. At 1265, your opponent will make mistakes if you give them problems to solve.
