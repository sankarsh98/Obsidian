# ♟️ Carlsen vs. Karjakin (2016) - Game 10
## *The Art of the Grind*

**Event:** World Championship Match (Game 10)
**Location:** New York City, USA
**Date:** November 24, 2016

---

## 📜 Historical Context

Magnus Carlsen was the defending World Champion, but Sergey Karjakin—nicknamed the "Minister of Defense"—was giving him the fight of his life. Going into Game 10, Carlsen was down in the match and desperately needed a win.

This game is legendary for its length and Carlsen's determination. What appeared to be a drawn Rook endgame turned into a grinding nightmare for Karjakin. After over 6 hours and 75 moves, Karjakin finally cracked.

**Match Score Before This Game:** Carlsen 4.5 - Karjakin 5.5

---

## 🏆 Why This Game Matters

1. **Match Situation:** Carlsen was losing the match. This was a must-win to stay competitive.
2. **The Grind:** Carlsen refused to accept a draw in a position most players would call equal. His persistence paid off.
3. **Psychological Warfare:** The sheer length and pressure of the game exhausted Karjakin, affecting his play in subsequent games.

---

## 📉 The Critical Moment

### Move 56... Nh7?

After 6+ hours of near-perfect defense, Karjakin finally made the decisive mistake. This passive Knight move allowed Carlsen's King to penetrate, and the game was over.

**Why was this a mistake?**
- The Knight on h7 is passive and cut off
- White's King gains entry via the center
- Black's Rook cannot stop both the King infiltration AND protect all pawns

---

## 🎯 Strategic Themes

### 1. Probing on Both Flanks
Carlsen repeatedly shifted his attack from the kingside to the queenside. This constant probing prevented Karjakin from setting up a stable defensive formation and eventually created weaknesses.

### 2. Creating Weaknesses
In Rook endgames, pawn weaknesses are critical. Carlsen maneuvered to fix Black's pawns on vulnerable squares, particularly on the kingside (h6, g6).

### 3. King Activity
Carlsen's King was an attacking piece, constantly looking for entry points. In the final phase, the King's penetration into the center was the decisive factor.

### 4. Psychological Endurance
Perhaps the most important theme. Carlsen trusted that in a 6+ hour game, his opponent would eventually err. Great players don't just calculate—they exhaust opponents.

---

## 🚦 Key Takeaways

1. **Play On in "Drawn" Positions:** What looks drawn to an engine may be difficult for a human to hold. Carlsen teaches us to never give up if there's any imbalance.

2. **Active King in Endgames:** The King is a fighting piece! Carlsen's King march decided the game.

3. **Two Weaknesses Principle:** Attack one side, then the other. When the opponent is stretched thin, exploit both weaknesses simultaneously.

4. **Physical and Mental Stamina:** This game was won as much by mental endurance as by chess technique.
