# ♟️ Lasker vs. Capablanca (1921) - Game 10
## *The Passing of the Crown*

**Event:** World Championship Match (Game 10)
**Location:** Havana, Cuba
**Date:** April 8, 1921

---

## 📜 Historical Context

Emanuel Lasker held the World Championship title for 27 years—the longest reign in history. Jose Raul Capablanca was considered chess perfection incarnate, a player so precise that he made chess look effortless.

This game came from their title match in Havana. Lasker, at 52 years old, was facing the peak of Capablanca's powers. This game shows why Capablanca was called "the chess machine"—pure technique, no wasted moves, inevitable victory.

**Match Score Before This Game:** Capablanca 4 - Lasker 0 (with 9 draws)

---

## 🏆 Why This Game Matters

1. **The Endgame Master:** Capablanca's handling of the Rook endgame is still studied today. He made winning look easy.
2. **The Isolated d-Pawn:** Lasker was saddled with an isolated d-pawn from the opening. Capablanca showed how to exploit this structural weakness.
3. **The Changing of the Guard:** This match marked the end of Lasker's era and the beginning of Capablanca's short but brilliant reign.

---

## 📉 The Critical Moment

### Move 43... Rb2!

Capablanca transitions into a Rook endgame that looks equal to a novice. But he calculated that his active Rook, better King position, and superior pawn structure would eventually force a win.

**Key Insight:** The position feels "drawish" but Capablanca understood that:
- White's King is passive (defending the d-pawn)
- Black's Rook is more active (penetrating on the 2nd rank)
- Black's pawn majority on the kingside will eventually create a passed pawn

---

## 🎯 Strategic Themes

### 1. The Isolated d-Pawn (IQP)
From the opening, White ended up with an isolated d4 pawn. In the endgame, this is a weakness because:
- It requires constant defense
- It ties down the defending pieces
- It can never be supported by another pawn

### 2. Piece Activity vs. Static Weaknesses
Capablanca kept his Rook active (on the 2nd and 7th ranks) while Lasker's Rook was reduced to passive defense. Activity trumps material and structure in Rook endings.

### 3. The Capablanca Technique
*"Do not fear simplification."* Capablanca traded down willingly because he knew the resulting endgame favored him. Many players shy away from endgames—Capablanca embraced them.

### 4. Patience
Capablanca maneuvered for over 20 moves without forcing anything. He improved his position move by move, waiting for Lasker to make a tiny inaccuracy.

---

## 🚦 Key Takeaways

1. **The IQP in Endgames:** An isolated pawn is dynamic in the middlegame but static and weak in the endgame. Guide the game toward endgames when you're playing against an IQP.

2. **Active Rook > Everything:** In Rook endgames, activity is king. A Rook on the 7th rank is worth more than an extra pawn in many cases.

3. **Don't Rush:** Capablanca didn't try to win immediately. He improved his position inch by inch until Lasker ran out of good moves.

4. **Trade When You're Winning:** Capablanca wasn't afraid to simplify into a winning endgame. Endgame technique is a weapon.
