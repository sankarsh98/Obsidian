## The Moves
**1. d4 d5 2. c4**

## The Concept
The Queen's Gambit is one of the oldest and most reputable openings in chess. White offers the c-pawn (a "gambit") to divert Black's central d5 pawn, aiming to establish superior control over the center with e2-e4 later. Unlike true gambits, Black cannot easily hold onto the pawn without creating positional weaknesses, allowing White to prioritize development and central domination.

> **Historical Note:** The Queen's Gambit has been played at the highest levels for over 500 years. Every World Champion has used it.

---

## Main Variations

### Queen's Gambit Declined (QGD): `2... e6`
*The classical response. Black reinforces the d5 pawn, maintaining a solid structure but locking in the light-squared bishop.*

**ECO Codes:** D30-D69

**Key Lines:**
- **Orthodox Defense:** 3. Nc3 Nf6 4. Bg5 Be7 5. e3 0-0 6. Nf3 Nbd7
- **Tartakower Variation:** 4. Bg5 Be7 5. e3 h6 6. Bh4 b6 (solves the bad bishop problem)
- **Cambridge Springs:** 4. Bg5 Nbd7 5. e3 c6 6. Nf3 Qa5 (attacks the Bg5)

---

### Queen's Gambit Accepted (QGA): `2... dxc4`
*Black takes the pawn to temporarily cede the center in exchange for open lines and free piece development. White usually regains the pawn comfortably.*

**ECO Codes:** D20-D29

**Key Lines:**
- **Classical:** 3. Nf3 Nf6 4. e3 e6 5. Bxc4
- **Central Variation:** 3. e4 (aggressive, sharp)

---

### The Slav Defense: `2... c6`
*A very solid choice where Black supports d5 with another pawn but keeps the diagonal open for the light-squared bishop, unlike in the QGD.*

**ECO Codes:** D10-D19

**Key Lines:**
- **Main Line Slav:** 3. Nf3 Nf6 4. Nc3 dxc4 5. a4 Bf5
- **Semi-Slav:** 2... e6 3. Nf3 Nf6 4. Nc3 c6 (combines French and Slav ideas)
- **Meran Variation:** 4. Nc3 e6 5. e3 Nbd7 6. Bd3 dxc4 7. Bxc4 b5

## Pros & Cons

| Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Central Control:** Fights immediately for the center. | **Theory Heavy:** Requires knowledge of many complex variations. |
| **Safety:** Solid structure, rarely risking a quick loss. | **Slow Paced:** Games can be long, grinding positional battles. |
| **Flexibility:** Can transpose into many other setups (Catalan, etc.). | **Locked Bishop:** In QGD lines, Black's light-square bishop is often passive. |

## 🏛️ Typical Pawn Structures

### The Carlsbad Structure (Exchange QGD)
```
White pawns: a2, b2, d4, e3, f2, g2, h2
Black pawns: a7, b7, c6, d5, e6, f7, g7, h7
```
*Occurs after White plays cxd5 exd5. White plans the **Minority Attack** (a3, b4, b5) to create weaknesses in Black's queenside. Black counters with piece activity and a potential kingside attack.*

### The Isolated Queen's Pawn (IQP)
```
White pawns: a2, b2, d4, e3, f2, g2, h2
Black pawns: a7, b7, c7, e6, f7, g7, h7
```
*White has an isolated d4 pawn—no adjacent pawns to support it. This provides open lines and attacking chances but can be a static endgame weakness.*

### Semi-Slav Triangle
```
White pawns: c4, d4, e3
Black pawns: c6, d5, e6
```
*Very solid for Black. The triangle of pawns is hard to break, but Black is cramped.*

## ⚔️ Middlegame Plans

### For White:
1. **The Minority Attack:** Push a3-b4-b5 on the queenside to create weaknesses (often in the Carlsbad structure)
2. **Central Breakthrough:** Play e3-e4 at the right moment to open the position
3. **Kingside Attack:** In some IQP lines, piece buildup and tactical strikes on the kingside
4. **Piece Pressure:** Rooks on the c-file and e-file, Knight outposts on c5/e5

### For Black:
1. **Solve the Bad Bishop:** Trade it via b6-Ba6 or Bd7-Be8-Bh5
2. **Activate Pieces:** Use the half-open c-file and put the Knight on e4
3. **d5-d4 Break:** Free the position by pushing the d-pawn (requires careful timing)
4. **Counterplay vs Minority Attack:** Prepare f7-f5 or direct kingside play

## Key Ideas

- **The Minority Attack:** In the Exchange Variation (where pawns are traded on d5), White often advances pawns on the queenside (a3, b4) to weaken Black's structure.
- **Isolated Queen's Pawn (IQP):** Several variations lead to an IQP for White. This provides open lines and attacking chances but can become a static endgame weakness.
- **Control of the e-file:** White often aims to place a Rook on e1 to support a central e4 push or control key squares.
- **The Bad Bishop:** Black's light-squared bishop (in QGD) is often blocked by pawns on e6/d5. Trading it is a key strategic goal.
