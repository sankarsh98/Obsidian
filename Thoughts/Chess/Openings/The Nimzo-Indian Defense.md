## The Moves
**1. d4 Nf6 2. c4 e6 3. Nc3 Bb4**

## The Concept
The Nimzo-Indian Defense is widely considered the most reliable defense against 1. d4. Instead of occupying the center with pawns (like in the QGD), Black controls the vital e4 square by pinning the White Knight on c3. This prevents White from playing e4 and establishing a full pawn center. It is a "hypermodern" opening that offers unbalanced positions without the high risks of the King's Indian Defense. If White avoids the pin by playing 3. Nf3, Black plays the Queen's Indian Defense (3... b6) or Bogo-Indian (3... Bb4+).

> **Historical Note:** Named after Aron Nimzowitsch, the hypermodern revolutionary. Virtually every top player has used the Nimzo-Indian at some point in their career.

---

## Main Variations

### The Rubinstein Variation: `4. e3`
*The most solid response. White simply develops the bishop to d3 or e2 and prepares to castle, accepting the pin for now.*

**ECO Codes:** E40-E59

**Key Lines:**
- **Main Line:** 4... 0-0 5. Bd3 d5 6. Nf3 c5 (fighting for the center)
- **Hübner Variation:** 4... 0-0 5. Bd3 c5 6. Nf3 Nc6 7. 0-0 Bxc3 8. bxc3 d6 (Black doubles the pawns)
- **Ragozin Fianchetto:** 4... 0-0 5. Bd3 d5 6. cxd5 exd5 7. Nge2 (quiet buildup)

---

### The Classical Variation: `4. Qc2`
*White moves the Queen to avoid doubled pawns if Black captures on c3. It aims to pick up the Bishop pair without structural damage.*

**ECO Codes:** E32-E39

**Key Lines:**
- **Main Line:** 4... 0-0 5. a3 Bxc3+ 6. Qxc3 b6 (White has bishops, Black has structure)
- **Zürich Variation:** 4... Nc6 (rare but interesting)
- **Modern Line:** 4... d5 5. cxd5 exd5 (fighting for the center immediately)

---

### The Sämisch Variation: `4. a3`
*White challenges the Bishop immediately. Black usually takes (Bxc3+), giving White the bishop pair but doubling White's c-pawns.*

**ECO Codes:** E24-E29

**Key Lines:**
- **Main Line:** 4... Bxc3+ 5. bxc3 c5 (attacking d4)
- **Keres Variation:** 4... Bxc3+ 5. bxc3 0-0 6. f3 (aggressive, preparing e4)

---

### The Leningrad Variation: `4. Bg5`
*White pins the Knight, preventing ...d5 for now. Sharp and tactical.*

**ECO Codes:** E30-E31

---

### The Kasparov Variation: `4. Nf3`
*Transposes to different systems, often Catalan or Queen's Indian territory.*

---

## Pros & Cons

| Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Prevention:** Stops White's plan of e4 comfortably. | **The Bishop Pair:** Black often has to give up the Bishop pair to damage White's structure. |
| **Flexibility:** Black can choose between closed structures or open games. | **Dependent:** You can only play it if White plays 3. Nc3 (must have a backup for 3. Nf3). |
| **Structure:** Black often plays against White's doubled c-pawns (a static weakness). | **Complex:** Requires understanding deep positional concepts (hanging pawns, weak squares). |

## 🏛️ Typical Pawn Structures

### Rubinstein with Doubled Pawns
```
White pawns: a2, c3, c4 (or doubled c2+c3), d4, e3
Black pawns: a7, b7, c5/c6, d5, f7, g7, h7
```
*White has doubled c-pawns but the bishop pair. Black targets c4/c3 and aims to blockade.*

### Classical (No Doubled Pawns)
```
White pawns: a3, b2, c4, d4, e3
Black pawns: a7, b6, c5, d5, e6
```
*White's structure is intact but Black has ...Bb7 controlling the long diagonal.*

### Sämisch Pawn Storm
```
White pawns: c3, c4, d4, e4, f3 (after f3 + e4)
Black pawns: c5, d6, e5
```
*White builds a big center, Black attacks it with ...c5 or ...f5.*

## ⚔️ Middlegame Plans

### For White:
1. **Use the Bishops:** Open the position to let the two bishops dominate
2. **e4 Break:** Eventually push e4 (the goal Black is trying to prevent)
3. **Kingside Attack:** In Sämisch, push f3-e4 and attack the king
4. **Control d5:** Place pieces on the strong d5 square

### For Black:
1. **Attack the Doubled Pawns:** Double rooks on the c-file, pressure c4
2. **Blockade c5:** Put a Knight on c5 to dominate the position
3. **Control e4:** Never let White comfortably play e4
4. **The d5 Break:** Challenge the center when the time is right

## 🔀 Transpositions

| From | To | Moves |
|------|-----|-------|
| Nimzo-Indian | Queen's Indian | 3. Nf3 instead of 3. Nc3; Black plays 3... b6 |
| Nimzo-Indian | Bogo-Indian | 3. Nf3 Bb4+ (instead of 3. Nc3 Bb4) |
| Nimzo-Indian | Catalan | 4. g3 transposes to Catalan territory |
| Nimzo-Indian | QGD | After 4. e3 d5, structures can resemble QGD |

---

## Key Ideas

- **Doubled Pawns:** If Black plays ...Bxc3, White gets doubled c-pawns. Black's entire strategy often shifts to blockading and attacking these weak pawns.
- **Control of e4:** This is the holy grail. Black uses the Knight on f6, Bishop on b4, and pawn on d5 (later) to stop White from ever playing e4 safely.
- **The Bishop Pair:** White's compensation for the bad structure is usually the Bishop pair. White tries to open the position to let the bishops shine.
- **Flexible Plans:** The Nimzo-Indian leads to many different structures; adaptability is key.

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes for Black

1. **Trading Too Early:** Don't play ...Bxc3 without a good reason
2. **Ignoring e4:** Always watch for White's e4 break
3. **Passive Defense:** The Nimzo requires active piece play to work
4. **Forgetting the Backup:** If 3. Nf3, you need a Queen's Indian or Bogo-Indian repertoire
