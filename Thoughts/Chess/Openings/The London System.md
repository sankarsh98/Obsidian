## The Moves
**1. d4 d5 2. Bf4**

## The Concept
The London System is a "system" opening, meaning White aims for the same piece setup regardless of what Black plays. White develops the dark-squared bishop to f4 *before* playing e3, ensuring it isn't trapped inside the pawn chain (unlike the Queen's Gambit). White then builds a solid pyramid of pawns (c3, d4, e3) and develops pieces to natural squares (Nf3, Bd3, Nbd2). It is low-maintenance, extremely solid, and avoids memorizing deep theoretical lines.

## Main Variations
*   **Classical London:** `1. d4 d5 2. Bf4 Nf6 3. e3`
    *   *Description:* The standard setup. White builds the pyramid and prepares for a slow squeeze.
*   **Jobava London:** `1. d4 d5 2. Nc3 Nf6 3. Bf4`
    *   *Description:* A more aggressive, modern interpretation. White blocks the c-pawn with Nc3 to play for quick piece activity and attacks on the center/kingside.
*   **London vs King's Indian:** `1. d4 Nf6 2. Bf4 g6`
    *   *Description:* Black fianchettoes. White usually plays h3 and Bh2 to preserve the bishop and attacks the kingside with h4-h5.

## Pros & Cons

| Pros | Cons |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Universal:** You can play it against almost anything (d5, Nf6, f5). | **Passive:** Can be too passive if White doesn't know how to attack later. |
| **Low Theory:** Understanding the plans is more important than move orders. | **Equality:** Black can equalize fairly easily with accurate play. |
| **Safety:** Very hard to lose quickly with the London. | **Repetitive:** You get similar pawn structures in almost every game. |

## Key Ideas
*   **The "Pyramid":** White's pawns on c3, d4, and e3 form an unbreakable wall.
*   **The e5 Outpost:** White often plants a Knight on e5. Since the f4 bishop supports it, it's a thorn in Black's side.
*   **Kingside Attack:** If Black castles Kingside, White often directs the light-squared bishop (on d3) and Queen (via f3 or h5) towards the King.
